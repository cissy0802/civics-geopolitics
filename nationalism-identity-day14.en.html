Civics · Law · Geopolitics: Nationalism and Identity

July 13, 2026
Day 14
We've covered how states form and how they are governed. Today we ask a more foundational question: what makes millions of strangers willing to call themselves "us" — and to tax, vote, and even die for that "us"? The core answer is the nation, modernity's most powerful and most dangerous political glue: it can fuse strangers into a self-governing community, and it can grind differences into fault lines of conflict. We'll take apart four components: whether a nation is "born" or "made," why "nation" and "state" so often fail to align, on what grounds a group may demand independence, and the two faces of nationalism. Throughout we do mechanism analysis — no side-taking, no judgment of any country or group.

1. The Construction of Nations: Discovered, or Invented?The Construction of Nations

How It Works

On "where nations come from," scholarship has two opposing lines. Primordialism holds that a nation is rooted in ancient blood, language, religion, and shared memory — a natural existence prior to politics, into which a person is "born." Modern constructivism (modernism) holds instead that the nation is a product of the last two or three centuries, "manufactured" jointly by industrialization, print, mass education, and the modern state.

Constructivism rests on several pillars: Benedict Anderson called the nation an "imagined community" — members never meet one another, yet imagine they belong to one whole because they read the same newspaper and share an official language; Gellner argued that industrial society's need for a standardized, mobile workforce is what gave rise to nations built on a unified language and culture; Hobsbawm exposed many "ancient traditions" as recent "invented traditions." The middle-ground ethnosymbolism (Anthony Smith) reminds us that although modern nations are newly built, they often draw on real pre-modern ethnic ties rather than conjuring them from nothing.

Cases · Cross-National
  • The "manufacture" of language: many countries' "standard language" was, in modern times, selected from among many dialects and promoted by the state — the standardization of French and of Mandarin both went hand in hand with a modern education system that absorbed local speech into "the language of one nation." A nation's supposed ready-made boundary is often itself a constructed result.
Debate & Trade-offs

The constructivist logic: stressing that a nation "can be shaped" explains why national boundaries move and why identity can be mobilized, awakened, or dampened; it also guards against treating "our nation has always been this way" as an unquestionable natural fact. The primordialist / ethnosymbolist logic: it rebuts pure constructivism as too weightless — if a nation is entirely fiction, why do people die for it? Real language, faith, and generational memory have deep roots. The two sacrifice opposite things: pure constructivism may underrate the real weight of emotion, while pure primordialism may mistake historical contingency for eternal destiny.

Common Misconception

"Saying a nation is 'constructed' is the same as saying it's fake" — this is the biggest misreading. "Constructed" does not mean "false": money and law are equally social constructs, yet they really govern our lives. Once national identity forms, it is a real social force able to move hundreds of millions; pointing out that it has a historical starting point is not to deny it, but to understand how it works and why it can change.

💡 In one line: nations are mostly imagined communities "manufactured" by modern industrial society and the state, yet they often draw on real ethnic ties — "constructed" does not mean "unreal"; it is real that gives them their power. 🤔 To ponder: if national identity can be shaped by education and media, is it more like a "discovered" fact, or an ongoing "maintenance" project?

2. Nation vs State: Why the Two Rarely Line UpNation vs State

How It Works

In everyday speech "state" and "nation" are used interchangeably, but in political science they are two different things. A state is a political-legal machine: it has territory, borders, a government, a monopoly on legitimate force, and international recognition — the "governance apparatus" of Day 7. A nation is a group of people who see themselves as belonging together and wish to govern themselves in common — an identity, not an institution. The ideal of the nation-state is to make the two boundaries coincide perfectly: one nation, one state.

The trouble is that this ideal is almost never truly realized. Ethnic, linguistic, and religious distributions are mottled and interwoven, while political borders were often drawn by war, colonialism, and dynastic marriage — the two maps rarely align. This "mismatch between nation and state boundaries" is the root source of nationalist tension.

Cases · Cross-National
Type of MismatchMeaningTension It Produces

One nation, many statesOne nation split across bordersCross-border identity, unification demands, friction with neighbors
Nation without a stateStrong national identity but no state of its ownPersistent demands for autonomy or independence

There are several institutional designs for handling mismatch, each with a cost: federalism and regional autonomy (devolving power to areas where a group is concentrated, but possibly feeding centrifugal forces), consociationalism (elites of each group share power and hold mutual vetoes, but it can grow rigid), and civic assimilation (playing down ethnicity, stressing a unified citizenship, but possibly suppressing minority identity).

Debate & Trade-offs

The "national self-determination" logic: a nation with a shared identity ought to have a political unit of its own to run its own affairs — only then is rule legitimate and consistent with members' will. The "pluralist co-governance" logic: insisting on "one nation, one state" is nearly impossible amid interwoven groups, and forcing it only triggers population transfers, border conflict, even cleansing — better to let different nations coexist within one state. The two sacrifice opposite things: the former may slice the state into ever-smaller pieces, the latter may leave minorities long feeling "this is not my country."

Common Misconception

"Every state has one corresponding nation behind it" — inaccurate. The vast majority of modern states are multi-ethnic; the "nation-state" is an ideal model, not a general reality; conversely, a single nation may be spread across several states. Taking "state = nation" as the default blinds us to the most common and thorniest tensions in real politics.

💡 In one line: a state is a governance machine, a nation is an identity community; the ideal of "one nation, one state" is almost never realized, and the mismatch of the two boundaries is the master source of nationalist tension. 🤔 To ponder: when a state houses several nations, should it strive to fuse them into "one new nation," or let several nations each keep their identity under one roof? What is the cost of each path?

3. Secession: On What Grounds May a Group Demand IndependenceSecession & Self-Determination

How It Works

When the "mismatch between nation and state" reaches an extreme, secessionism appears. Its moral fulcrum is the "right to national self-determination": a nation has the right to decide its own political belonging. This principle was written into international consensus and the UN Charter after World War I (Wilson's "Fourteen Points") and amid the wave of decolonization.

But self-determination has, from birth, an embedded hard contradiction: it must contend with another equally sacred principle — state sovereignty and territorial integrity (Day 12). The mainstream approach in international law is to "tier" self-determination: colonial peoples and peoples under foreign occupation enjoy an "external self-determination" leading toward independence; while a group inside an already-independent state is usually recognized only for "internal self-determination" within existing borders (autonomy and cultural rights), not an unconditional right to independence. Only when a group suffers severe, systematic oppression and all other remedies have failed ("remedial secession") does independence become more likely to earn moral support.

Cases · Cross-National
  • Referendum path vs unilateral declaration: some states set a legal channel for separation — negotiation between center and region and a recognized referendum (as in Canada's framework for Quebec, requiring a "clear question and clear majority"); other separations occur by unilateral declaration of independence, whose international recognition often stays long in doubt. Whether the procedure is lawful deeply affects the legitimacy and stability of the outcome.
Debate & Trade-offs

The "consent equals legitimacy" logic: government legitimacy derives from the consent of the governed; if a group clearly and persistently no longer wishes to belong to a state, forcing them to stay means rule without a basis in consent. The "stability first" logic: if "leave whenever you're unhappy" holds, almost every multi-ethnic state could be split without limit, and a newly independent body often has its own minorities inside — so secession frequently creates new minority problems. The two sacrifice opposite things: an unlimited right to secede endangers all existing borders, while absolute territorial integrity can permanently lock in those who would rather leave.

Common Misconception

"National self-determination means every nation has the right to found a state at once" — an over-simplification of international law. Mainstream practice does not recognize an unconditional unilateral right to independence; in most cases self-determination points to autonomy and rights protection within borders, not automatic divorce. Equating self-determination directly with independence ignores the deliberately placed balance between it and territorial integrity.

💡 In one line: secessionism's fulcrum is self-determination, but it clashes naturally with territorial integrity; international law "tiers" self-determination, mostly recognizing only internal autonomy — unilateral independence is not a universal right. 🤔 To ponder: in judging whether an independence claim is legitimate, which standard should come first — "the majority's will right now" or "not creating a newly oppressed minority"? And when they conflict, what then?

4. Civic vs Ethnic Nationalism: Who Counts as "Us"Civic vs Ethnic Nationalism

How It Works

Nationalism is not monolithic; the key split between its two faces is "who qualifies to be a member of the nation":

  • Civic nationalism: the boundary is drawn by shared political values, law, and citizenship. So long as you embrace the constitution, obey the law, and take part in public life, you can be "us" regardless of blood, language, or religion. It is in principle open and joinable, bound by shared institutions and commitments.
  • Ethnic nationalism: the boundary is drawn by inborn blood, language, religion, and ancestry. Membership is closer to "given at birth," resting on descent rather than choice. It is strongly cohesive but closed; outsiders can hardly truly "join."

Most countries' national identity is a blend of the two, differing only in proportion — the civic version is like an organization joined by contract, the ethnic version like a family inherited by birth.

Cases · Cross-National
  • Two logics in nationality law: jus soli (born here, a citizen) is closer to civic thinking, while jus sanguinis (a citizen only if your parents are citizens) is closer to ethnic thinking. Many countries' nationality law is a differing mix of the two, adjusted with immigration politics — the abstract contrast thereby becomes concrete institution.
Debate & Trade-offs

The civic version: open, able to integrate immigrants, compatible with liberal democracy; but critics argue that relying purely on abstract values makes its cohesion weak, unable to answer "why exactly are we one family?" The ethnic version: emotionally deep, quick to unite, offering a strong sense of belonging; but it is inherently exclusionary, apt to define minorities and immigrants as "eternal outsiders," sowing seeds of discrimination and conflict. The two paths sacrifice opposite things: one may err toward "thin," the other toward "exclusion."

Common Misconception

"Civic nationalism = good, ethnic nationalism = bad" — this moral dichotomy is too crude. Civic nationalism is not naturally mild: it too can exclude the different in the name of "defending our values"; ethnic ties are not only negative, carrying real cultural and emotional identity. The two are, in truth, two different ways of drawing a boundary, each with integrative power and exclusionary risk — what matters is how it is used.

💡 In one line: nationalism's core split is "who counts as us" — the civic version draws the line by shared values and law and is open in principle, the ethnic version draws it by descent and is cohesive but exclusive; reality is mostly a blend. 🤔 To ponder: for a society continually joined by immigrants, is "shared values" alone a strong enough bond? And if you add cultural and emotional depth, how do you keep it from sliding into exclusion of "outsiders"?

Going Deeper

1. If nations are mostly "constructed," why can one inspire greater willingness to sacrifice than many "real" interests?
Because once construction succeeds, it generates a real structure of meaning: the nation plugs a finite individual life into a grand narrative that "existed before me and will outlast me," offering belonging, dignity, and a sense of immortality that material interests cannot. Precisely because it answers a deep human need for meaning and belonging, it can mobilize sacrifice more powerfully than many concrete interests.
2. For a multi-ethnic state, under what conditions is each of assimilation, federal autonomy, and consociational power-sharing more likely to succeed?
There is no universal optimum; it depends on structure. Assimilation is more feasible where ethnic differences are small and minorities dispersed, but provokes backlash from a large, concentrated, strongly self-aware group. Federal/regional autonomy suits geographically concentrated groups, at the cost of possibly feeding centrifugal forces. Consociational power-sharing suits several evenly matched groups whose elites are willing to cooperate, at the cost of rigid decision-making. In reality it is usually a mix of the three.
3. Can civic nationalism's two goals — staying open and holding enough cohesion — be reconciled?
One approach is "thick civic identity": not only assent to abstract clauses but also cultivating shared historical narrative, public ritual, and mutual responsibility, adding emotional depth to the civic bond while keeping the entrance open to all willing to accept those commitments. The difficulty is proportion: thick enough to cohere, yet not so thick that a "cultural threshold" becomes ethnic exclusion in disguise.
